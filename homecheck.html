01 · Context & opportunity

A product born from the business.

Unlike a project that starts from a user pain, HomeCheck came from the business: Tinsa was looking for new lines that could add value inside the appraisal process. The starting idea — take advantage of the fact that a professional is already inside the home doing the valuation to capture a few extra data points and offer the buyer a report on the condition of the property they're about to purchase: electrical system, heating/cooling, cracks and damp, safety and access, orientation and comfort, planning status.

The real problem wasn't interface, it was definition: turning a business hunch ("let's capture a bit more data and add value") into a concrete product that is sellable, technically viable and honest with the buyer. That was exactly my challenge — bringing the idea down to earth.

02 · Why it mattered

An entry product that moves a bigger sale.

USR

For the buyer

They're making one of the most expensive decisions of their life with little information about the home's real condition. HomeCheck gives early signals — including a heads-up on likely future repair costs — at a very accessible price (€50–60).

BIZ

For Tinsa

A new revenue line, but above all a hook product. HomeCheck is designed to open the door to the full inspection — a far more expensive report, with on-site visits by in-house technicians and precise instrumentation, run by the company Revicasa.

A strategic consequence worth being clear about from the start: HomeCheck's success isn't measured so much by itself as by what it moves downstream. It's an entry product that uses a privileged channel — the appraisal already in motion — to activate a bigger sale.

HomeCheck (hook, €50–60) → full inspection by Revicasa. The metric that really matters lives downstream.
03 · Constraints & starting point

Unusual rules that shaped the design.

This project was defined by uncommon constraints — and handling them well is a good part of its merit.

SCP

The appraiser is not a technical inspector

They could only capture what's verifiable by eye, photo or even smell (e.g. burning signs in the electrical system). Any instrumental measurement was out of scope — reserved for Revicasa.

TIM

The appraiser's time is sacred

Their goal is to spend the least time possible on a visit; any extra load hits their productivity directly. HomeCheck couldn't make the visit heavier.

4×

Four distinct surfaces

Work spanned the appraiser app (Vega), a back office (Nova Admin), the ecommerce (Nova) and the Mi Casa app — plus a generated PDF. Each surface with its own technical dependencies.

LAW

Legal responsibility

Stating the condition of a home forces careful language: neither over-alarming nor committing Tinsa to diagnoses that can't be sustained by a visual review.

MTD

No safety net

Small team, no PO — and a critical point: we had to define and design the product at the same time, with no prior spec to build on.

DAT

Data is always captured

The report had to be sellable even after the appraiser had left the home, so data capture happens on every visit — whether or not the report was sold.

04 · My role & collaboration

Orchestrating expertise that wasn't mine.

My work had two sides worth separating: product (bringing the business idea down to earth — which fields to capture, which not, who fills them and how they appear in the report) and design (the technician's back office, the PDF report and its layout, the capture flow in the appraiser app, and the upsell screens in Nova and Mi Casa).

Collaboration was the core of the project:

1

With knowledge & the technical profile: I don't have the criteria to know what an appraiser can or can't assess, so the split of fields came from constant dialogue — I brought product and UX criteria (minimise the appraiser's load, keep capture fluid, use the photo as a bridge), they brought the technical criteria of what data is capturable and by whom.

2

With business: hand in hand to scope the work and translate the idea into something tangible.

3

With development: feasibility validation on each of the surfaces.

Single source of truth

A fields spreadsheet that held the whole project together

The artefact that sustained all this collaboration was a fields spreadsheet I designed as the single source of truth: each field with its conditional logic, its data source (appraiser or technician), its options and the comment the customer would read.

05 · Exploration, decisions & trade-offs

Honest value and a commercial hook.

Decision 01

Split the data between appraiser and technician, with the photo as the bridge

The design principle was to minimise the appraiser's load. The solution: the appraiser captures only two or three quick data points and many guided photos; later, from the office, the technician derives the rest of the data from those photos. One decision solving three problems at once — it keeps the visit light, lets a non-inspector produce a report with technical criteria, and makes the product sellable even after the visit. To make the photos truly usable, I designed capture with guided modals (what to photograph, how, and how many shots per element).

Impact: a single field split became the backbone of the whole service.

Decision 02

Scope to what's visually verifiable

We deliberately decided HomeCheck would only include what's checkable by image or sight, leaving instrumental inspection to Revicasa. An honest trade-off — and, on top of that, the one that holds up the hook model.

Trade-off: anything needing instruments is out of scope, by design.

Decision 03

Tone and traffic lights as a product layer

I designed a traffic-light scoring system (🟢🟡🔴) with pre-written comments for each state. The copy is the safety layer: it never over-alarms and, on sensitive items (cracks, damp), includes warnings clarifying it isn't a definitive diagnosis and recommending a technician or the full inspection. That's where the tension honest value ↔ commercial hook resolves: the report adds real value and the upsell is born from the honest caveat, not from hiding information.

Why it works: the language carried tone, user value and legal responsibility at once.

Decision 04

Conditional logic and calculated scoring

The form adapts with conditional logic so irrelevant fields never show (less load, fewer errors). And for blocks like orientation and comfort, I designed a normalised calculation that turns simple inputs (orientation, window size, shading, distance to buildings) into indicators the customer can understand (sun exposure, ventilation, comfort).

The real difficulty: defining and designing at the same time produced many versions and shifting fields. The product didn't stabilise until we closed the fields and consolidated the spreadsheet as the source of truth.

06 · The solution

Four surfaces and one final deliverable.

HomeCheck is a product spread across four surfaces and a final deliverable:

1

Appraiser app (Vega): a capture flow for the few contextual data points plus guided photo-taking, designed to be fast and unobtrusive in the field.

2

Technician back office (Nova Admin): the technician reviews the uploaded photos, completes and validates the interpretive fields, and generates the report with one button.

3

PDF report (the B2C deliverable): structured for the end customer with a per-block traffic-light summary and, below it, the detailed sections (electrical, heating/cooling, safety & access, cracks & damp, orientation & comfort, planning) with comments and recommendations in plain language.

4

Sales surfaces: upsell screens in Nova (Tinsa's web ecommerce, offered alongside the appraisal purchase) and in the Mi Casa app (for those who bought the appraisal but not HomeCheck).

07 · Evolution of the solution

A spec that came alive while building.

The most relevant evolution here happened during construction itself, not after launch. Having to define and design in parallel meant the product was made through iteration: multiple versions, fields coming in and out, and a lot of uncertainty until the spec consolidated. The spreadsheet's "change log" is the trace of that living spec.

The turning point was making that spreadsheet the single source of truth: from there, every surface could align on one shared definition. The product launched in late May 2026, so the real-usage evolution is still ahead.

The product didn't stabilise until the spreadsheet became the single source of truth — then every surface aligned on one definition.
08 · Impact & learnings

Shipped recently — impact in progress.

In the interest of honesty: HomeCheck launched very recently and there's no usage data yet. The impact is therefore ongoing, and this case study rests on the delivered product, the decisions and the method.

How we'll measure success

Defined, still without data

Number of reports sold — not for the revenue volume, expected to be modest, but as a signal of real user interest. And impact on full-inspection sales, the metric that really matters because HomeCheck is a hook — measured both on Tinsa's own web and on Revicasa's, whose sales data we have access to.

✦

Defining and designing at once is chaotic; what stabilises the process is a single artefact as the source of truth. Today I'd create it sooner.

✦

Designing for different roles across different surfaces forces you to think in service, not screens: the split of responsibilities is the most important design decision.

✦

Copy can be a product layer. The traffic-light language solved tone, user value and legal responsibility all at once.

09 · Reflection

Design that starts before the screen.

If I ran the project again, I'd try to time-box and close the field definition before starting to design — or at least assume from day one that the spreadsheet-as-source-of-truth had to exist before any screen. It would have saved us quite a few versions.

What I take away is that good Product Design doesn't always start with a screen: sometimes it starts by turning a fuzzy business idea into a defined product — orchestrating expertise that isn't yours, designing for several actors at once, and making sure user value and the commercial goal don't cannibalise each other, but hold each other up.
