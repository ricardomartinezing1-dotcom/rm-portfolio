01 · Context & problem

A tool that was useful but painful.

Vega already had a place in surveyors' daily routines — the habit didn't need creating from scratch. But the experience was penalising the very thing the app existed to do: help people work faster.

The research made it clear. The app was seen as useful but problematic: slow loading times, errors and crashes, an interface that was hard to use and poorly suited to real field conditions. A revealing symptom was that it didn't update in real time, which broke the surveyor's trust in what they saw on screen. The result: some were turning to external tools to cover needs the app wasn't handling well.

We didn't start from hunches. The problem was grounded in real research: functional analysis of the app, stakeholder interviews across business, ops and technology, 6 surveyors interviewed, one observed field visit, and a user survey.

02 · Why it mattered

In appraisals, time is the business.

The faster a visit and its data capture, the more assignments a surveyor can take on. A slow and fragile tool wasn't just an inconvenience — it was a direct drag on the whole network's productivity.

USR

For the user

Less friction and less time lost on tasks that add no value, in a mobile job with time pressure and sometimes no signal.

BIZ

For Tinsa

An internal tool that was dragging the network's efficiency and losing ground to alternatives that surveyors were using on the side.

The migration to Flutter — designed to unify the two apps into one — was the lever that opened the window. The opportunity wasn't to rewrite the app, but to redesign it: use the fact that we were touching everything to fix what was really hurting.

Help surveyors organise and manage their assignments quickly and efficiently, cutting time spent on tasks that add no value.
03 · Constraints & starting point

The rules of the game that shaped the design.

TEC

Technical & architecture

Front built in Flutter with Material Design; business logic lived in Tinsa's backend, connected to other systems (Pickup, Valentino, antecedents…). Real-time updates were an inherited technical problem, not a UI quirk.

ORG

Organisational

Development was split between Tinsa's backend lead and an external front-end team. Designing without aligning both first meant risking proposals that couldn't be built.

€/T

Budget & timeline

Limited scope. Not everything the research flagged as valuable could fit in the project.

USR

User profile

The surveyor network averages over 45 years old and over 90% work individually. The design baseline had to respect that real profile.

04 · My role & collaboration

No decision was made alone.

Three-way prioritisation. Decisions weren't negotiated only with the PO: business had significant weight. My job was to advise and push — with research evidence upfront — what mattered most: things that were blocking app use or generating high friction. Usually the three parties reached agreement.

Technical validation before designing. We set up a process for every feature that prevented designing in a vacuum:

1

Technical meeting with backend (Tinsa) and frontend (external) to understand real constraints.

2

Quick sketch to check feasibility.

3

Only then, high-fidelity mockups.

Domain knowledge. The knowledge lead was essential for defining the app's logic and edge cases — rules an external designer can't invent and that condition every appraisal flow.

05 · Exploration, decisions & trade-offs

Balancing user, business and feasibility.

Decision 01

Invalidate the "more data" hypothesis

Business believed value would come from letting surveyors fill in valuation data from the app. Research showed the opposite: surveyors want to spend the least time possible on a visit. The path wasn't asking for more input — it was automating and reducing effort.

Impact: reoriented the entire product strategy towards automation and friction reduction.

Decision 02

Design for the "classic individual" profile

Research identified four profiles (tech-savvy/classic × individual/group). With such different profiles, designing for one risked penalising the rest. Agreed with business: prioritised the classic individual because they represented the network's reality — average age 45+, 90%+ solo workers.

Trade-off: group and tech-savvy features (batches, routes) moved to the back of the queue.

Decision 03

Evolve the information architecture, not rebuild it

Mapping the information revealed that part of the existing IA wasn't used or wasn't useful. Instead of a radical restructure, we refined what existed using research evidence, removing what added nothing.

Why: given the timeline and budget, a full restructure carried more risk than value.

Conscious trade-offs. We'd have liked to go deeper on navigation to get an even cleaner interface, but there was no time or budget. And features the research flagged as needed — batch management and incident reporting — were cut by prioritisation and cost. Not oversights: deliberate scope decisions.

06 · The solution

Starting from the foundations.

First, a UI Kit. I built a UI Kit based on the new corporate aesthetic (Accumin) and Material Design. Having the visual system solved before redesigning screens gave the rest of the work coherence and speed.

Then, a screen-by-screen redesign — applying fundamental UX principles, restructuring information hierarchy, and making every view simultaneously more pleasant and more useful.

The two hardest design challenges

Remove, not add

The assignments list: each card carried too much information. The hard work was cutting — keeping only what was essential to read status and priority at a glance.

The assignment detail: it concentrates a lot of sensitive, critical information. The challenge was ordering it so the important parts were accessible without noise.

Tangible changes: new interface with refreshed hierarchy and iconography, a new navigation model, a noticeably faster app, and — critical for trust — improved real-time updates, one of the most frustrating inherited failures.

07 · Evolution of the solution

The case of the "Visit Mode".

Adoption of the change. User feedback on the impact was positive, though some found the initial transition hard: relearning how to navigate a tool they used daily creates an adjustment curve. A useful reminder that, in internal tools, the cost of change for the user is also a design variable.

Business and knowledge proposed being able to advance valuation work from the mobile. I applied what research had taught us: I went back to ask users, who didn't see it as a priority. I shared that evidence with the team. Even so, given its value for business, the decision was made to proceed.

My job then was to design it as quickly and linearly as possible, building on capabilities the app already had (like geo-referencing). Today Visit Mode is in production and its adoption isn't bad.

Bring the evidence, defend it — and when business decides otherwise for legitimate reasons, commit and execute as well as possible.
08 · Impact & learnings

Once reliable and useful, surveyors came back.

−25%
Data capture time — the core task is now faster.
+15%
Volume of data captured — faster and more productive.
+35%
App usage time — surveyors centralise work they used to do elsewhere.
Reading note

+35% usage doesn't contradict saving time

Each task is faster (−25%) and more productive (+15%); the +35% reflects greater adoption, not more effort. Measured the other way it'd be a vanity metric; in this order, it's a signal that the app earned its place.

✦

Validating technical feasibility before falling in love with a solution saves redesigns and frustration.

✦

In internal tools, the cost of change for the user is part of the design, not a side effect.

✦

Defending the evidence and knowing when to commit when business decides otherwise is part of the craft.

09 · Reflection

Finding the intersection.

If I ran the project again, I'd fight to reserve time and budget for deeper work on navigation: it was the lever for an even cleaner interface and it was left half-finished because of scope. Batch management and incident reporting also remained pending — pieces with clear value that each needed their own discovery.

What I take away is the confirmation that good Product Design isn't about imposing the "best" solution — it's about finding where the real user need, the business objective, and what the team can actually build intersect. And defending the evidence with judgement inside that intersection.
