The challenge

A redesign driven by law, data and feedback.

Given Radar's evolving legal landscape and its need to adapt based on user feedback and data analysis, we were tasked with conducting a structural analysis and redesign of the application.

The company & the tool

Backed by 30 years of property valuation.

Radar is a powerful tool developed by Tinsa, a leading property valuation company in Spain, with over 30 years of experience.

+720
Valuations per day
+20
Countries around the world

Radar is a useful tool for real estate professionals, investors, and anyone interested in Spain's housing market. It provides a privileged view of the market through refined data and infographics.

It works by selecting the town and housing type, drawing a polygon on the map, applying filters like room count, and generating an interactive market study in seconds.

Project context

Three lenses for the redesign.

After a first analysis and feedback from our customers, we knew there were three perspectives we had to approach the redesign from. The case study process is structured around each of these.

Initial problems

What was holding the app back.

01

Fast development

The app had to be rebuilt multiple times in a short period due to legislative changes — leaving information architecture and usability debt behind.

02

Growth of information

Continuous data growth meant some sections contained excessive amounts of information, making it hard to extract meaning at a glance.

03

Layout problems

Rapid iterations introduced layout inconsistencies that accumulated across screens, breaking any sense of structure.

04

Bulky UX

Without a heuristic approach, Radar had accumulated numerous small usability issues that added friction to common tasks.

05

Unclear infographics

The data increase meant the existing infographic structure could no longer handle the load cleanly — visuals lost their clarity.

Business goals

What success would look like.

01

Engage new users

A good way to measure success is attracting new users who might be interested in what the product offers.

02

Make users more frequent

Higher usage frequency would also support research and usability efforts going forward.

Analysis & research

Understanding the information from the inside.

To understand the problem, the first step was a thorough analysis of the app — what information was available, how it was presented, how users navigated it, and what the existing hierarchy was and why. The result was a detailed sitemap covering every section with an explanation.

Due to project constraints, card sorting with real users was the only primary research we could run. Three sources informed the information architecture work: card sorting results, user feedback, and Hotjar data.

Card sorting

The process needed to be an open card sort — we needed to see how users organised information independently of the existing blocks.

Each team member (PO, UX designer, analyst, developer) ran a separate card sort, with the goal of surfacing internal biases and comparing them to what real users produced.

Hypothesis

All sections can be grouped into two categories

By analysing the app's information, we can organise every section under two main headings:

Supply & Demand
  • Valuation Table
  • Most Recent Properties for Sale
  • Distribution of Supply
  • Max / Median / Min Offer Prices
  • Real Estate Demand in the Area
  • Latest Sold Properties
Market Indicators
  • Financial Information
  • Housing Price Trends
  • Real Estate Zoning Scoring
  • Price Forecast
  • Variation & Target Price Range
  • Gross Return on Investment
