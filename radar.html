Worked during 2020

Radar

Redesigning the future of market research.

The challenge

Given Radar's evolving legal landscape and its need to adapt based on user feedback and data analysis, we were tasked with conducting a structural analysis and redesign of the application.

§ Context

The company & the tool.

Radar is a powerful tool developed by Tinsa, a leading property valuation company in Spain, with over 30 years of experience.

+720Valuations per day
+20Countries around the world

Radar is a useful tool for real estate professionals, investors, and anyone interested in Spain's housing market. It provides a privileged view of the market through refined data and infographics.

It works by selecting the town and housing type, drawing a polygon on the map, applying filters like room count, and generating an interactive market study in seconds.

Project context

With a first analysis and feedback from our customers, we knew there were three perspectives we had to approach the redesign from: information architecture, layout, and infographics. The case study process is repeated from these three points.

§ Initial problems

What was holding it back.

Five recurring issues kept surfacing across user feedback and internal analysis — each pointing to a deeper structural debt.

  • Fast development

    The app had to be redone multiple times in a short period due to changes in legislation, leaving information architecture and usability problems behind.

  • Growth of information

    Continuous data growth meant some sections contained an excessive amount of information, making it difficult to understand at a glance.

  • Layout problems

    Rapid iterations introduced layout inconsistencies that piled up across screens, hurting the sense of structure.

  • Bulky UX

    Without a heuristic approach, Radar had numerous small usability issues that added friction to common tasks.

  • Unclear infographics

    Due to the increase in data, the existing infographic structure could no longer handle it cleanly.

§ Business goals

What success would look like.

Engaging new users

Business is business — a good way to measure the success of our work is to attract new users who might be interested in our product.

Make more frequent users

Having more frequent users would also help us with research and usability tasks down the line.

§ Analysis & research

Three perspectives on the same product.

With a first analysis and feedback from our customers in hand, we set up the redesign around three threads: information architecture, layout, and infographics.

Information architecture

To understand the problem, the first step was to conduct a thorough analysis of the app — what information was available, how it was presented, how a user navigated through it, and what the current hierarchy was. The result was a detailed sitemap that included every section with a written explanation.

Due to the external conditions of the project, we were only able to run a card sorting activity with real users. The three sources of insight feeding the architecture work were card sorting results, user feedback, and Hotjar data.

Card sorting

It was necessary for the process to be an open card sorting: we needed to see how users organised the information independently of the current information blocks.

Each team member (PO, UX designer, analyst, developer) conducted a separate card sorting activity in parallel — the goal was to identify the biases inside our own team and compare them against what real users produced.

Hypothesis

By analysing the app's information, we can group all sections into two main categories:

Category 01Supply & Demand
Category 02Market Indicators

The Supply & Demand category would include sections on Valuation Table, Most Recent Properties for Sale, Distribution of Supply, Maximum / Median / Minimum Offer Prices, Real Estate Demand in the Area, and Latest Sold Properties.

The Market Indicators category would include Financial Information, Housing Price Trends, Real Estate Zoning Scoring, Price Forecast, Variation and Target Price Range, and Gross Return on Investment.
