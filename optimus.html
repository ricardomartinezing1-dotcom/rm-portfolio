How to read this

Stewardship, not a single challenge → solution arc

Optimus is a living, massive product with no real "end". I joined when it was already in motion and have sustained and evolved it as lead designer. So I tell it as stewardship + three representative cases that show the kind of problems I solve within it.

01 · Context — the beast

A hyper-complex, fragile system.

Optimus is the desktop application used to produce property valuations at Tinsa. And the world of valuation is wild: a single tool has to account for homes, industrial warehouses, parking spaces, commercial premises… an enormous range of cases. On top of that sits the regulatory layer: the full ECO methodology, every admissible valuation method, and constant oversight from the Bank of Spain.

The result is a hyper-complex, fragile system: because of its size and how interconnected it is, touching one area can easily break others. I joined in 2022, when it had already been in development for four or five years. I didn't start it — my challenge was to step into a running machine, understand it, and evolve it without stopping it.

02 · Why it mattered

Competing against inertia.

Optimus wasn't competing against a market rival: it was competing against inertia. The tool it was replacing was very old, very large legacy software that many appraisers had used for twenty years. Replacing that muscle memory is one of the hardest adoption challenges there is.

BIZ

For Tinsa

A modern, maintainable tool that, above all, passes the Bank of Spain's regulatory scrutiny.

USR

For the appraiser

A better way to work — as long as the change didn't penalize two decades of habit.

What was at stake wasn't a screen; it was the operational continuity and regulatory compliance of the entire valuation business.
03 · Constraints & starting point

The permanent rules of the game.

FRG

Fragility through complexity

Every change requires tightly scoping the work and understanding what it affects — it's easy to break something downstream.

REG

Regulation

Designs don't just have to be usable: they have to comply. Usability is built on top of a framework that isn't negotiable.

INH

Inherited, in-flight

I joined with no blank slate, facing a very steep learning curve, advancing through small, tightly scoped tasks.

1×

Sole designer

The only design person on the tool: no design system, no one to lean on to build one.

USR

Veteran users

A base accustomed for years to the previous software, which turns every change into a real adoption cost.

04 · My role & collaboration

From mentored to lead.

I started out mentored by the lead designer already in place; as he moved on, I became the lead designer of the application. Given the learning curve, the method was to move forward gradually, with very tightly scoped tasks. The work rests on close, constant collaboration:

1

Domain-knowledge area: side by side, always. They're the domain authority — they tell me whether a design meets appraisers' real needs and the regulations. Over time I began helping them map the implications of new casuistry and define requirements.

2

Engineering: feasibility validation of every proposal, and alignment with their existing component library.

3

Product Owner: to prioritize and push each task through.

My contribution, beyond the screens, was to translate a brutally hard domain into a coherent visual language, and to raise the product's craft.

05 · Case A — Commercial premises

Adding a property type that didn't exist.

The challenge. Adding commercial premises as a valuable property type to a system that didn't account for them. The hardest part wasn't drawing screens: it was studying the entire casuistry and analyzing which parts of the application would be affected and which wouldn't. That mapping alone took a long time, and landing everything else depended on it.

Decision 01

Registering a premises, with its three types

Modelling the process knowing there are three differentiated types of premises, and that the floor/height they sit on enables one class or another.

Decision 02

Fitting it into the interface

Its interaction with other property types — for example a valuation that combines a home and a premises: what the primary use is, when it's possible, and in which cases it isn't allowed.

Collaboration: continuous alignment with the domain team on every case and on how to bring it into the interface while respecting the way Optimus conceives a valuation.

Why I'm proud of it. For learning from an expert team on such a complex topic, and for translating that complexity into a simple result: a user understanding clearly what they need to do to value a commercial premises. In a system like this, simplicity is the hard-won achievement.

06 · Case B — Valuations by state

One dropdown that reconfigures everything.

The challenge. Allowing the same home to be valued according to its state radically changes how the valuation works. In project, there's often no site visit; under construction, there's a visit but you can't access the interior because the property doesn't yet exist as such. That forces a reconfiguration of the flow itself, not just the content.

The interface & data challenge. Many fields had to be swapped for new ones, fitting new forms into slots that already existed. A simple state dropdown (completed / under construction / in project) caused large parts of the interface to change in a cascade.

The hardest part

Human orchestration, as demanding as the technical work

Getting business (which also had opinions), the domain team, engineering and the design perspective to agree.

The trade-off. An interface that reconfigures itself by state implies a learning curve for the user; we managed it to keep that curve as small as possible, and I think it ended up well resolved.

07 · Case C — A visual system from scratch

A UI kit that became a shared system.

The challenge. There was no design system. The previous designer reused whole screens manually. As the only designer, without a reusable foundation I was going to be far too slow.

The decision. To build, on my own, a simple but solid UI kit, applying best practices and aligned with the component library engineering already had. The underlying intent: to raise the product's bar, not just replicate what existed.

The scope it gained

One visual system across three applications

What started as a tool to move faster became a system shared across Optimus, Pickup (the appraiser's job-management tool) and Jazz (the review department's tool). I anchored it in Optimus, which had the best interface, and reused it in the other two — which I also design.

Why it counts. It's a different kind of contribution: not solving a case, but craft and systemic ownership. It sped up design, brought coherence across products, and raised the visual standard in a sustained way.

08 · Impact & learnings

Impact that compounds.

Because this is a living product, impact is cumulative and measured in several ways:

REG

Audits passed

In a regulated tool, passing the Bank of Spain's audits is itself a success metric.

MIG

Legacy migration

With no external competitor, the challenge was to displace inertia: let users choose and watch adoption, then business progressively blocked actions in the old software so they could only be done in Optimus.

ADO

Share of transactions

Adoption and share of valuations run in Optimus vs. Tinsa's total. It won't reach 100% — very specific casuistry stays manual — but the trend is the reference point.

FBK

User feedback

Behavioral analytics on specific flows (Hotjar) and feedback collection — positive.

✦

In a fragile, complex system the craft lies in tightly scoping the work and validating constantly. Prudence is a design skill.

✦

Humility and translation are senior competencies: learning from domain experts and turning complexity into something simple is most of the value I bring here.

✦

Raising craft compounds over time. A dedicated visual system paid off in speed and coherence for years.

09 · Reflection

Success isn't a launch — it's sustained health.

Designing Optimus taught me that, in products like this, success isn't a launch, it's sustained health: the tool continuing to grow, comply, and gain adoption year after year without breaking. It's a less flashy kind of work than a redesign from scratch, but closer to what mature product design actually demands.

What I take away is the value of surrounding yourself with people who know far more than you about an impossible domain, having the humility to learn from them and the discipline to translate all of it into something a user understands at first glance. And, looking back, I'd have invested in the visual system even earlier: the sooner that foundation exists, the faster and more coherent everything that follows becomes.
